The problem

Two structural roles. One was invisible.

Standard betweenness centrality identifies the routing hub — the individual who sits on the most paths between others. Remove a routing hub and long-distance communication in the group collapses.

But there is a second role that betweenness cannot see: the quality anchor — the individual whose immediate neighbors are all tightly connected to one another. Remove a quality anchor and the group's long-distance communication is fine. But the local neighborhood quietly deteriorates.

These are two distinct failure modes. For forty years we had a metric for only one of them.

RC = (Rhythm × 0.4) + (Coherence × 0.4) + (Bridging × 0.2)  —  same formula across all substrates below.

Rhythm × 0.4 How many connections does this individual have, relative to the maximum possible?
Coherence × 0.4 How tightly connected are this individual's direct neighbors to one another?
Bridging × 0.2 How often does the network route shortest paths through this individual?

Four substrates · one question

Does this system have the structure to stay stable?

RC does not require that nodes be animals or that edges be social bonds. It requires only that entities form connections and that connections carry meaning. The same question recurs across every substrate below.

Substrate 02 · Theoretical

Acoustic Frequency Compatibility

Before animals form social bonds, they interact acoustically. The hypothesis: frequency compatibility is not a consequence of existing bonds — it is a prior condition determining which bonds can form at all. RC scores may reflect acoustic history, not only current position.

Substrate 04 · Pipeline Ready

Particle Physics Decay Networks

B-meson decays produce three-body final states. Standard CP violation analysis asks: do B+ and B− decay with different frequencies? RC asks a structurally different question: do their decay networks have different coherence structure? A differential RC result above null would be orthogonal to existing measurements.

LHCb B→KKK · CERN Open Data record 4900 · pipeline validated on simulation

Open question: does CP violation have a structural signature?

CP violation — the asymmetry between matter and antimatter — was discovered through particle counting: more B+ than B− decays survive. This tells us that the asymmetry exists. It does not tell us whether the topology of matter and antimatter decay networks differs structurally.

A differential RC analysis across B+ and B− decay networks asks exactly that question. The pipeline is built and validated on LHCb phase-space simulation data (CERN Open Data record 4900). The null result from the simulation — no structural asymmetry, as expected — validates that the pipeline is sensitive to real asymmetry if it exists.

If a real data run returns Z > 3 above a charge-shuffle null model: CP violation has a coherence signature, not only a numerical one. That result would be orthogonal to every existing CP violation measurement.
